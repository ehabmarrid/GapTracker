## Introduction

As part of this course, we chose to develop a product that addresses an acute problem among students: **the accumulation of hidden academic knowledge gaps**. While many solutions exist for task management and time management, there is no dedicated tool for managing "understanding status." When these gaps are not treated in real time, they contribute to cognitive overload, exam anxiety, and impaired academic performance.

---

## 1. Academic Research Summary on the Target Population

**Target population:** Undergraduate students in higher education institutions, with emphasis on knowledge-intensive disciplines such as engineering and computer science (STEM).

We conducted comprehensive research on the population and the findings are organized by the following dimensions:

### Cognitive and Learning Aspects

Research indicates that students struggle to maintain continuity of understanding over time ("forgetting curve"). Missing a single foundational topic creates a "knowledge gap" that prevents understanding of advanced material. The central problem is that most students identify gaps too late — usually only during exam periods, when time for correction is limited.

### Mental and Emotional Aspects

Uncertainty about one's level of material mastery is a factor that leads to stress and academic anxiety. The "Imposter Syndrome" phenomenon is very widespread and causes students to feel that "everyone understands except me," leading to avoidance and reluctance to cope.

### Social Aspects

Many students avoid exposing their lack of understanding due to fear of social judgment — a fear of being perceived as weak in front of lecturers or in classroom groups. Therefore, there is a need for a discreet and personal tool that does not require public exposure.

### Environmental Aspects

The modern student learns in varied environments: frontal classroom, online learning, library, and commuting. The frequent transitions make it difficult to maintain organized tracking of the learning flow, and a gap is created between the moment of "lack of understanding" (in class) and the moment of "completion" (at home).

---

## 2. Product Goals — What Primary Problem Does It Solve?

The primary problem is **the difficulty of identifying, managing, and reducing knowledge gaps in real time**. Application goals:

1. **Identification (Capture):** Allow quick and immediate marking of points of unclear understanding mid-lesson.
2. **Management and Prioritization:** Converting the feeling of "I don't know anything" into a defined and managed task list.
3. **Overview:** Providing an up-to-date status picture (Dashboard) on the readiness level in each course before exam periods.
4. **Mental Load Reduction:** Creating a sense of mastery and capability through material breakdown.

---

## 3. Product Usage Environment

The product is designed for hybrid use adapted to the student lifestyle:

- **In class / lecture (Mobile):** Quick, minimalist use for real-time gap documentation without disrupting attention to the lesson.
- **At home / library (Web/Desktop):** Work environment for learning management ("Deep Work"), completing gaps, viewing statistics, and planning revision.
- **On the road (Mobile):** Quick refresher and memory recall during dead times (commutes).

## 4. User Survey — Key Insights

Within the user research framework, an anonymous online survey was conducted among **36 undergraduate students** from diverse disciplines. The survey was structured as a focused multiple-choice questionnaire designed to examine: how academic understanding gaps are identified, students' ability to define them, the tools they currently use for learning management, actual coping patterns with understanding gaps, and preferences regarding the simplicity of digital learning tools. The survey findings served as a central basis for consolidating insights about user needs and validating the proposed concept.

### When Students Identify Gaps

Survey findings indicate that understanding gap identification mostly occurs at a relatively late stage in the learning process:

- **~44%** of respondents reported identifying understanding gaps mainly when solving exercises
- **~22%** identify them close to exam periods
- Only **~28%** of students noted they identify understanding gaps during the lesson itself

These data indicate that for the majority of students, knowledge gaps are not identified in real time but rather accumulate over time and are discovered at a stage where the opportunity to complete them in a calm and thorough manner is limited.

### Difficulty Defining the Gap

An additional finding from the survey highlights significant difficulty in the precise definition of the lack of understanding:

- **~64%** of respondents said it is not easy or is difficult for them to define precisely what their learning gap is — and that the feeling is usually general ("I don't understand the whole topic")
- Only **~17%** of students reported that it is very easy for them to point to a specific point that is unclear

This finding highlights students' difficulty in translating a general feeling of lack of understanding into a focused problem that can be addressed effectively.

### Gap Between Existing Tools and Student Needs

Regarding existing learning management tools, the survey indicates wide use of journals, apps for task management, and notes. With that, ~70% of respondents reported that the tools they currently use barely help or do not help at all in managing understanding gaps, focusing mainly on managing technical tasks such as submission deadlines. This highlights a significant gap between existing market tools and students' needs in knowledge and understanding management over time.

### Gap Between Awareness and Action

An additional central finding is the gap between awareness of understanding gaps and actual action:

- When asked how they cope with understanding gaps after identifying them, **~53%** of students noted they defer treatment of the gap — intend to return to it but forget or ignore it and move on
- Only **~39%** reported that they document and actually treat the gap

This finding points to the absence of a convenient and quick documentation mechanism for real-time knowledge gap treatment, which leads to procrastination and the accumulation of gaps throughout the semester.

### Preference for Simple Tools

Finally, the survey points to a clear preference for simple and intuitive learning tools:

- **~83%** of respondents noted that it is important or very important that a learning tool will not require unnecessary time investment or ongoing maintenance
- **~97%** of students would consider using a dedicated tool for managing academic understanding gaps, provided it is easy to use and naturally integrates into the learning process

In summary, the survey findings strongly reinforce the need for developing a dedicated tool for managing and reducing academic knowledge gaps. Such a tool needs to allow quick identification and documentation of understanding gaps, assist in their focused definition, bridge between awareness and actual action, and be minimalist, simple, and low-maintenance.

## 5. Persona Definitions

Based on the research, we defined primary and secondary users:

### Persona 1: Dana — The Rookie Student (Primary User)

**Profile:** 23-year-old first-year engineering student, achieving, outward-facing, with perfectionist tendencies.

**Needs:** Must know she hasn't missed anything. Needs order and confidence.

**Product Use:** Will use the app to document every unclear point in class in order to "calm her conscience" and deal with it at home.

## 6. User Interviews — Findings

Within the user research framework, three users representing the different personas defined in the previous stage were interviewed. The goal of the interviews was to deepen understanding of the motivations, goals, behaviors, knowledge level, and current experience of users in the domain of learning management and understanding gaps. The interviews allowed examining how students currently cope with knowledge gaps, what work processes they perform day-to-day, and what are the central difficulties they encounter.

### Persona 1: Dana — The Rookie Student (Primary User)

**Profile:**
Dana, 23, a first-year engineering student. She experiences a high academic load and demonstrates a tendency toward perfectionism and pressure.

**Motivation and Goals:**
Dana's primary driver is reducing the feeling of pressure and uncertainty about her understanding status. She aspires to maintain order and control in her learning, and to know with certainty that she hasn't "missed" important topics. Her central goal is to arrive at the exam period feeling prepared and confident.

**Behavior and Knowledge:**
Dana currently uses unorganized notes and memory to track unclear topics. She identifies understanding gaps retroactively but finds it difficult to define them precisely in real time. Her knowledge level in basic learning management tools is basic, and she does not use complex tools due to a high learning curve.

**Current Experience and Work Processes:**
Dana's academic experience is described as reactive — "firefighting." During the lesson she mentally marks problematic points to herself, but usually does not return to them systematically. Her primary tasks are understanding lecture material and completing gaps at home.

**Main Difficulties:**
Dana's primary difficulty is the lack of ability to systematically track her understanding gaps and know which topics require immediate treatment. The absence of a structured tool increases the feeling of load and anxiety.

---

### Persona 2: Omar — The Working Student (Primary User)

**Profile:**
Omar, 26, a third-year student, works part-time alongside studies and suffers from chronic lack of time.

**Motivation and Goals:**
Efficiency is Omar's central motivation. He cannot allow himself to study all the material in depth, so his central goal is to know what is critical for the exam and what he can defer or skip.

**Behavior and Knowledge:**
Omar mainly uses task management apps to track deadlines. He is aware that he has knowledge gaps, but usually defers dealing with them until close to an exam or the weekend. His knowledge level of digital tools is relatively high, but he avoids tools that require frequent maintenance.

**Current Experience and Work Processes:**
Omar's learning takes place in short and fragmented intervals. He performs primary tasks such as solving exercises and studying in a focused manner for exams, but without an overall picture of his understanding level in the course.

**Main Difficulties:**
The central difficulty is the lack of ability to intelligently prioritize knowledge gaps. Omar finds it difficult to know which topics will meaningfully affect his exam success.

---

### Persona 3: Ronit — The Tutor (Secondary User)

**Profile:**
Ronit, a tutor in an introductory academic course, responsible for reinforcing student understanding and clarifying complex topics.

**Motivation and Goals:**
Ronit's motivation is improving teaching quality and her adaptation to students' needs. Her central goal is to understand which topics have widespread understanding gaps among students.

**Behavior and Knowledge:**
Ronit currently relies on classroom questions and informal feedback to identify difficulties. However, this information is partial and does not necessarily represent all students.

**Current Experience and Difficulties:**
The central difficulty is the lack of objective and aggregated data on the classroom's understanding status. The absence of such a status picture makes it difficult to adapt teaching in real time.

---

### Interview Findings Summary

The interviews show that a widespread problem exists among different users: lack of ability to manage understanding gaps in a systematic, accurate, and consistent manner. Both students and academic staff operate reactively without a tool that allows documentation, prioritization, and reflection of understanding status. These findings reinforced the need for a dedicated knowledge gap management tool and served as a direct basis for designing the concept and prototype in the continuation of the work.

## 7. Future Use Scenarios

### Scenario 1: "Quick Mark"

During a solo lecture, the lecturer explains a complex topic. The student feels he lost focus. He opens the app, taps the "new gap" button, records a short voice note ("I didn't understand the proof of the theorem"), photographs the board, and saves. All this within 10 seconds so he can return to listening.

### Scenario 2: "Studying for the Exam"

Two weeks before the exam, the student enters the course "dashboard." The system shows him that he has 12 open gaps in this course, 3 of which are critical. He selects a critical gap, studies the topic, and marks "completed." His readiness score rises.

## 8. Competitive Analysis

Since there is currently no dedicated solution for managing academic knowledge gaps, competitive analysis was conducted against common alternative solutions that students actually use. The selection of competitors was based both on a market review and on user survey findings, which indicated wide use of apps for knowledge management, task management, studying tools, and informal solutions such as notes.

### Competitors Analyzed

#### 1. Notion — Knowledge and Summary Management

Notion is a powerful and flexible platform for knowledge management, summaries, and information organization. It allows creating complex structures and content visualization. However, it requires a lot of setup time and ongoing maintenance. As a result, it is not suitable for quick use during a lesson or immediate documentation of understanding gaps.

#### 2. Monday / Todoist — Task Management

These applications serve for managing tasks, deadlines, and task prioritization. Very efficient for managing task load, but lack academic context. Understanding gaps are translated into general tasks ("submit exercise") without representation of understanding status or connection to course material.

#### 3. Anki — Study via Flashcards

Anki is an effective application for knowledge drilling via scheduled flashcards and repetitions. However, it focuses on fact memorization and does not provide an answer for managing complex understanding processes, identifying gaps, or mapping course material.

#### 4. Notes (Physical or Digital)

Notes are a very common and accessible solution among students, as survey findings show. They allow quick documentation of unclear points, but suffer from lack of organization, no prioritization, difficulty tracking over time, and lack of ability to get an overall status picture of understanding level.

### Insights from the Analysis

The analysis of existing solutions shows that each provides only a partial answer to students' needs. Notion provides depth and flexibility but suffers from operational overhead. Todoist and Monday excel in task management but lack academic context. Anki supports drilling but does not support complex understanding processes. Notes allow quick documentation but lack structure, prioritization, and reflection of understanding level.

In contrast, Gap Tracker combines the speed and convenience of documentation (like notes), the ability to prioritize and focus (like task apps), and academic depth and knowledge status reflection — and precisely fills the Gap identified in the user research and survey.
